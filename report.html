NSF TIP Knowledge Graph — Guide

NSF TIP Programs Knowledge Graph — A Guide to the Tool and How to Read It

What this document is: a plain-language guide to the interactive NSF TIP Knowledge Graph (TIP_Knowledge_Graph.html). It explains what you are looking at, what every control on the screen does, and how to use the tool to answer real questions. You do not need any technical background to follow it. Open the HTML file in any web browser; it is one self-contained file and needs no internet connection.

1. What the Knowledge Graph is, in one minute

The NSF Directorate for Technology, Innovation and Partnerships (TIP) runs dozens of programs. It is hard to see, at a glance, how they relate — which ones feed into each other, which fund the same researchers, which serve the same kinds of organizations. This tool turns that mess into a visualization you can explore.

  • Every circle/node is a program.
  • Every line/edge between two circles means “these two programs are related,” and the color of the line tells you why.
  • Some edges are arrows — those show a direction: program A leads into program B.
  • The controls on the left let you filter, search, and switch between different ways of looking at the same graph.
  • The panel on the right shows the details of whatever circle or line you click.

It is one connected graph. The most important design choice: the view leads with operational and structural relationships — how programs actually connect — and keeps looser “these sound similar” relationships available but turned off until you ask for them.

2. What is in the graph

58 programs in total, in two tiers:

  • 41 TIP-led programs — the programs TIP actually runs. These are the colored circles and the focus of the tool.
  • 17 cross-directorate “context” programs — programs like GRFP, REU, CAREER, and MRI that appear in TIP’s funding feed but are run by other parts of NSF. They are included for context but de-emphasized: smaller, gray, dashed circles, hidden until you ask for them.

The 41 TIP-led programs are grouped by TIP’s three strategies, plus a cross-cutting group:

Strategy Meaning Circle color
Strategy 1 Accelerate critical and emerging technology Blue
Strategy 2 Expand the geography of innovation Green
Strategy 3 Build a competition-ready workforce Orange
Cross-cutting Spans more than one strategy Brown
Context Cross-directorate, not TIP-led Gray (dashed)

3. What the connecting lines mean

A line means two programs are related, and its color says why. There are seven kinds, deliberately split into two groups. A connection is never a hunch — every line traces to a specific, checkable source, and you can click any line to read it.

Group A — Operational & structural (shown by default)

These describe how programs are actually connected.

Color Kind What it means Example
Blue arrow Directed pathway One program leads into another (a real, one-way dependency) I-Corps Teams → PFI (PFI supports proof-of-concept work by I-Corps participants); SBIR/STTR → Test Bed (Test Bed is open to SBIR awardees)
Pink Program family / lineage One program is part of, or grew out of, another I-Corps Teams ↔︎ Hubs; POSE → PESOSE
Red Solicitation reference One program’s official announcement names the other PESOSE’s announcement references the security of open-source ecosystems (Safe-OSE)
Orange Operational attribute The two programs share a specific operational feature both require an industrial partner; both serve MSIs; both are Ideas Labs

Group B — Similarity & discovery (off by default)

These are looser “they resemble each other” signals — useful for exploration, but not evidence of an operational link. Turn them on with the checkboxes when you want them.

Color Kind What it means Example
Purple Award co-occurrence The two programs funded the same people/institutions I-Corps and PFI share 60 lead researchers
Teal Text similarity Their announcements use similar language POSE and Safe-OSE both center on open-source ecosystems
Cyan Shared technology domain Both work in the same technology area Breaking Low and VINES both work on advanced wireless

Two visual cues on every line: thickness = strength (a higher score; see Section 4), and color = the strongest evidence when a pair has more than one kind (click to see them all). Directed pathways are the exception — they are drawn as arrows and kept separate (Section 4).

4. The “synergy score” and the directed pathways

Most lines carry a synergy score from 0 to 1. A higher score means more independent kinds of evidence point the same way — not that any single link is ironclad.

The score combines evidence with diminishing returns: each extra signal raises it, but by less each time, so no single signal dominates. This is the standard noisy-OR rule for fusing independent evidence — the score is one minus the product of each layer’s “miss” (1 − weight), i.e. the chance that at least one of the signals is real (see Judea Pearl, Probabilistic Reasoning in Intelligent Systems, 1988, §4.3). It assumes the layers are roughly independent — an approximation here — so the number is best read as a ranking, not a literal probability. Click a line and the right panel shows the exact arithmetic, e.g.:

score = 1 − (1 − 0.26) × (1 − 0.216) × (1 − 0.155) = 0.51

Read the per-layer confidence (High / Medium / Low) over the decimal. A tidy “0.51” can come from three weak hints that happen to agree; the panel always breaks it down so you are not misled.

Directed pathways work differently. Because a pathway is inherently one-way (A leads into B), it is not folded into the two-way score. Each pathway is its own blue arrow, scored by the confidence of its quoted evidence, and drawn solid for high-confidence, dashed for medium.

5. Lifecycle and provenance (where each program stands today)

Click a program and you get a lifecycle badge that tells you its current funding state and where the evidence came from — without opening any web page. Across the 58 programs:

Lifecycle status Meaning
Open solicitation An active solicitation is accepting proposals (badge shows the NSF number and next deadline)
Between cycles The solicitation expired and is awaiting reissue, or is closed to new proposals
Archived solicitation A past solicitation, no longer active
Dear Colleague Letter The program operates via a DCL rather than a full solicitation
Initiative / program page Described on an NSF initiative page; no standalone solicitation
No standalone solicitation A pilot, roadmap effort, or partner-operated challenge with no NSF solicitation

The badge also shows a provenance line (“3 source documents · authority: NSF” or “· source: external partner/operator”), so you always know whether a program’s information comes from NSF itself or an outside operator. You can filter the whole graph by lifecycle status (Section 6).

6. The control panel on the left, top to bottom

Everything here changes what is shown, never the underlying data.

Search box

Type a program name, acronym, technology, or source. The graph narrows to matches. Example: quantum, or wireless to find Breaking Low and VINES.

“TIP strategy” drop-down

Show only one strategy (Accelerate Technology / Expand Ecosystems / Build Workforce) or Cross-cutting. Example: Strategy 3 isolates the workforce programs.

“Domain” drop-down

Filter to one of the tool’s descriptive technology tags. Example: Open-Source Ecosystems → POSE, PESOSE, Safe-OSE.

“Key Technology Area (statutory 10)” drop-down

Filter to one of the 10 official Key Technology Areas from the CHIPS and Science Act (Artificial Intelligence; Advanced Computing and Semiconductors; Quantum Information Science and Technology; Robotics and Advanced Manufacturing; Disaster Prevention and Mitigation; Advanced Communications; Biotechnology; Data and Cybersecurity; Advanced Energy and Industrial Efficiency Technologies; Advanced Materials). The government-defined view of which technology each program serves.

“Lifecycle status” drop-down

Filter by funding state — e.g., show only programs with an Open solicitation, or only Archived ones. (See Section 5.)

“Minimum synergy score” slider (default 0.35)

Hides lines weaker than the value set. Drag right for only the strongest relationships; left (toward 0.15) to reveal weaker, more speculative ones.

“Relationship evidence” check-boxes

Turn each line type on or off. On by default (the operational/structural group): Directed pathways, Program family / lineage, Solicitation references, Operational attributes. Off by default (the discovery group): Award co-occurrence, Text similarity, Shared technology domain. Example: to see only proven directional links, leave Directed pathways on and uncheck the rest.

“Program display” check-boxes

  • Show cross-directorate context programs — brings in the 17 gray, dashed non-TIP programs.
  • Mute high-volume programs (SBIR/STTR, I-Corps, PFI) — hides the three giants so smaller programs’ connections stand out.
  • Show Key Technology Area hubs (statutory 10) — adds 10 gold triangle hubs; programs cluster around the KTAs they serve.
  • Show domain, strategy, and stage hubs — adds the descriptive grouping nodes.

“Fit” and “Pause physics” buttons

Fit re-centers and zooms to the whole graph. Pause physics freezes the gentle auto-arranging motion (it settles and freezes on its own a second after loading).

Legend and “Network” summary

The legend is the color key for the seven line types, the gold KTA hubs, and the gray context programs. The summary gives a live count of visible programs, visible lines, and their average score as you filter.

7. The detail panel on the right

Click a program (circle): full name; strategy or context status; whether it is high-volume; its lifecycle + provenance badge; its Key Technology Areas (gold pills, or “Cross-cutting — no single statutory KTA”); its descriptive domains; its sources with clickable links; notes; and a ranked list of its top relationships.

Click a relationship (line): the two programs; the synergy score; the plain-English explanation of how that score was computed (the formula with the numbers filled in); and each piece of evidence by layer — its kind, weight, confidence, and the underlying detail (e.g., “Shared PIs/CoPIs: 4; shared institutions: 12,” or the exact quoted sentence). For a directed pathway, the panel states the direction and shows the quote, and notes that the arrow exists only because the row was accepted during curation.

8. Worked examples — answering real questions

  • “Trace the translation pathway.” Leave Directed pathways on (uncheck the others if you like). The arrows show the real, evidence-backed flow — e.g., I-Corps Teams → PFI, I-Corps Teams → Translation to Practice, SBIR/STTR → Test Bed.
  • “Which programs have an open solicitation right now?” Set Lifecycle status to Open solicitation.
  • “Which programs require an industrial partner?” Turn on Operational attributes and look for the orange links (or click a program and read its relationships).
  • “Which programs work on quantum?” Set Key Technology Area to Quantum Information Science and Technology (NQVL, Quantum + X; QLCI too if the context tier is on).
  • “Show the smaller programs without the giants dominating.” Check Mute high-volume programs.
  • “What sounds similar even if not formally linked?” Turn on Text similarity and Shared technology domain and lower the score slider.

9. Where the information comes from (so you can trust it)

Every connection is grounded in NSF’s own materials, not opinion:

  • Directed pathways are curated one by one from explicit solicitation language (e.g., “open to current and former SBIR/STTR awardees”); each carries a verbatim quote and was accepted by a reviewer. No quote, no arrow.
  • Operational attributes come from structured fields extracted from the full solicitation pages (eligibility, required partners, tracks/phases, target audience).
  • Family / lineage comes from the documented structure of the programs.
  • Solicitation references are quotations from the official announcements.
  • Award co-occurrence comes from NSF’s award records.
  • Text similarity compares the wording of the announcements.
  • Shared technology domain and the Key Technology Area tags come from NSF’s own technology tagging where available, and program descriptions otherwise.
  • Lifecycle and provenance come from the solicitation feed and the downloaded source pages.

Click any line and the panel shows the specific source behind it.

10. Things to keep in mind

  • The tool is for seeing structure and ranking relationships, not precise measurement. Treat the score as “stronger vs. weaker,” and lean on the per-layer confidence.
  • The directed-pathway layer is deliberately sparse. Only a handful of programs have a stated one-way dependency; most do not. An arrow is high-signal precisely because it was only drawn where the text demanded it.
  • Newer programs show fewer lines. Programs without award history (PESOSE, VINES, AI-Ready America) can only connect through shared attributes, text, or domain — not shared researchers, yet. Absence of a line can mean “no data yet,” not “no relationship.”
  • The context (cross-directorate) tier is intentionally faint — there for completeness, not as the focus.
